Key Terms Reference — Chapter 9

Non-parityWhen insurance coverage for behavioral health conditions is less generous than for other medical conditions. A long-standing problem in U.S. health insurance.
Carve-outsInsurance arrangements that outsource behavioral health coverage to a separate MBHO, creating a separate and often inferior benefit.
Managed Behavioral Healthcare Organization (MBHO)A subcontractor that assumes financial risk for and manages access to behavioral health services on behalf of an insurer.
Mental Health Parity Act (1996)Equalized lifetime and annual dollar limits but left loopholes for day limits, visit limits, and higher copays, and excluded substance use disorders entirely.
MHPAEA (2008)Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act. If a plan covers behavioral health, coverage must be at parity with medical/surgical benefits across all cost-sharing and treatment limitations.
Nonquantitative Treatment Limitations (NQTLs)Treatment limits that restrict benefits without numbers, such as preauthorization or step therapy. A common mechanism for avoiding full parity. Enforcement weakened in 2025.
Serious Mental Illness (SMI)Mental illness that substantially interferes with major life activities. Approximately 5.2% of U.S. adults. The highest-need population in behavioral health.
ComorbidityTwo or more diagnoses in the same individual. Nearly half of those with any psychiatric disorder meet criteria for two or more disorders.
DeinstitutionalizationThe large-scale discharge of patients from state psychiatric hospitals from the 1960s onward, without sufficient community resources to support them.
Recovery-Oriented Systems of Care (ROSC)A strength-based, person-centered model that replaced diagnosis-anchored problem lists with individualized hope plans centered on empowerment and choice.
Essential Health Benefit (ACA)Ten categories of services that individual and small group marketplace plans must cover, including behavioral health treatment. Coverage is required, not just parity.
Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALYs)A measure of total disease burden. Neuropsychiatric disorders rank first in the U.S., surpassing cardiovascular disease and cancer.

Can you spot a parity violation?

For decades, insurance companies treated mental health and substance use disorder coverage differently — and worse — than coverage for physical health. Laws like MHPAEA (2008) changed that, but loopholes and enforcement gaps remain.

⚠ 2026 Policy Context — What Has Changed Since the Textbook

ACA Subsidy Cliff Returned (January 1, 2026): Enhanced marketplace subsidies expired December 31, 2025. Average premiums more than doubled for millions of enrollees. Households earning even $1 over 400% of the federal poverty level — about $62,600 for a single person — lost all premium tax credits entirely.

Medicaid Cut by $1 Trillion (H.R. 1, July 4, 2025): The One Big Beautiful Bill Act cut federal Medicaid funding by approximately 15% over 10 years. The CBO estimates 11.8 million people will directly lose Medicaid coverage. Since Medicaid pays 25% of all U.S. behavioral health spending, these cuts directly threaten access to care for the most vulnerable populations.

MHPAEA Enforcement Weakened (May 2025): The current administration announced it will not enforce strengthened NQTL regulations finalized in September 2024. Those rules would have required insurers to prove comparable NQTL application using independent medical standards. MHPAEA itself remains law, but enforcement of the strongest new protections has stalled. Where noted in scenarios below, this context matters.
