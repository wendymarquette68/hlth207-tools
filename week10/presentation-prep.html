From Letter to Presentation

Do Not Repeat Your Letter. Improve It.

The senator has already read your letter. Your presentation is your opportunity to take what you wrote and make it more specific, more compelling, and more actionable. Here is how to address each gap from your letter feedback.

Six Upgrades That Matter Most

🎯 Population Specificity

If your letter said "youth" or "patients," your presentation must name your population with at least two defining characteristics. A senator cannot allocate funding to a vague group.

Ask yourself: What is their age? Where do they live? What is their income level or insurance status? What specific risk are they facing?

📊 Evidence Interpretation

Every statistic needs a "so what." After every piece of evidence, explain what it means and why it matters to your policy argument. Data does not argue for itself.

Use this formula: State the statistic, then write "This demonstrates that..." and connect it directly to your recommendation.

🏛️ Policy Process Knowledge

Your presentation must show you understand who has the authority to make the change. Name the right level of government and the right agency.

Is this a federal issue (Congress, HHS, CMS) or a state issue (Maryland Department of Health)? What type of action is needed: a law, a regulation, or a funding allocation?

⚖️ Actionable Recommendations

Vague recommendations like "increase funding" are not actionable. Every recommendation must answer four questions before it is considered strong.

Who will act? What exactly will they do? How will it be enforced or monitored? How will it be funded or supported?

💼 Professional Tone

Read your presentation aloud. If any sentence sounds informal or vague, rewrite it. You are presenting to a legislative committee, not texting a friend.

Replace "until it is cured" with "to ensure appropriate and ongoing management of the condition."

📚 APA Accuracy

Every in-text citation must have a matching entry in your reference list, and vice versa. Missing or incorrect APA formatting undermines your credibility.

Check: Author, Year, Title, Source, DOI or URL. Every source you cite must appear in your reference list with correct formatting.

Before vs. After: Policy Recommendation

Too General

"We need to increase funding for mental health services for young people."

This tells the senator nothing about who will act, what they will do, or how success will be measured. It cannot be translated into policy action.

Strong and Actionable

"I recommend that Congress allocate funding through HHS to require standardized mental health screening protocols in Maryland schools serving Title I populations, with compliance monitored through annual federal reporting requirements."

Who acts: Congress. What: allocate funding through HHS. For what: standardized screening in specific schools. Enforcement: annual federal reporting.
