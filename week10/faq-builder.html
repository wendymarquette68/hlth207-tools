🎯 Your Target Population

Be specific. Vague terms like "youth" or "patients" will cost you 20 points.

💡 Why specificity mattersA senator cannot allocate funding to "young people." You need to describe exactly who you are advocating for: their age, where they live, their income or insurance status, and the specific health risk they face.
Step 2 of 7

Section 1: What Is the Problem?

This becomes the opening of your presentation. Describe the healthcare problem, explain why it exists at a system level, and connect your first research finding. This is where your senator decides whether to keep listening.

❓ The Problem — Opening Section

📋 What goes into your presentation from this sectionSlides: Use this as your Introduction slide and your Problem slide. Video/Podcast: This is your opening 60 seconds. Handout: This becomes your first FAQ answer.
📊 Evidence RequirementEvery section must include at least one piece of evidence from your article reviews, followed by an explanation of what it means. Use this formula: State the finding. Then write "This demonstrates that..." and connect it directly to your argument.

Section 2: Who Is Affected?

This is where your senator sees the human cost. Describe your target population in specific detail and explain what this problem means for their real lives. Use evidence from your second article review.

👥 Who Is Affected — Population Section

Step 4 of 7

Section 3: What Does the Research Show?

This is where you establish your credibility. Synthesize all three article reviews. Show the senator that the evidence is clear and that inaction is not acceptable.

🔬 The Research — Evidence Section

📋 What goes into your presentation from this sectionSlides: This is your Evidence slide or slides. Video/Podcast: This is your research segment. Handout: This becomes your third FAQ answer. All three of your article reviews must appear somewhere across your evidence sections.
Step 5 of 7

Section 4: Your Policy Recommendation.

This is the most important section of your presentation. Worth 25 points. A strong recommendation answers four questions. If any one is missing, the recommendation is not actionable and senators cannot act on it.

🏛️ Who Has the Authority to Act?

Before you write your recommendation, identify the correct level of government and the right agency. This determines everything else.

📋 What goes into your presentation from this sectionSlides: This is your Policy Gap slide. Video/Podcast: Transition from evidence to solution. Handout: Opening of your fourth FAQ answer.

⚖️ Your Policy Recommendation

Fill in all four pillars. Every one must be specific. This is the core of your senator presentation.

✅ Strong vs. WeakWeak: "Increase funding for mental health." — This scores near zero. Strong: "Congress should allocate funding through HHS to require standardized mental health screening protocols in Maryland Title I schools, with compliance monitored through annual federal reporting tied to Title I funding eligibility." — This scores full points.

📣 Your Call to Action

This closes your presentation. Tell the senator exactly what you are asking them to do. Be direct and specific. This is not a summary. It is your ask.

📋 What goes into your presentation from this sectionSlides: This is your final slide. Video/Podcast: Your closing 30 seconds. Handout: Your call to action box.
